Mapping the Jagged Frontier

Work in progress

Anyone who has ever prompted a language model or launched a coding agent has experienced this. You ask Claude Code to implement a full-stack app and it delivers a genuinely impressive prototype; then Sonnet, upon being asked whether you should walk or drive to the car wash that’s ten minutes away on foot, cheerfully recommends a nice stroll. Already in 2023, Ethan Mollick and his colleagues at MIT Sloan gave this phenomenon of high (and often unpredictable) variability a name, the jagged frontier, which they defined as “the nonintuitive strengths and weaknesses of AI performance relative to human performance, and how it is changing over time,” but the idea that automation doesn’t progress uniformly and predictably is even older. Moravec’s paradox, the observation that reasoning tasks we consider “hard” are often computationally cheap, while sensorimotor tasks we consider “easy” are enormously expensive, dates to the 1980s. Large language models have worsened this paradox rather than resolving it. Tasks that feel like they should be trivially automatable, such as common-sense reasoning or basic spatial inference, remain stubbornly out of reach; meanwhile, tasks that feel like they should require deep expertise—from writing legal memos to synthesizing large bodies of research—fall well within the frontier.

So, what does the frontier look like today? Picture it as a jagged, blobby shape, sometimes extending far beyond human capability, sometimes falling embarrassingly short. Only the most fanatic detractors of LLMs will not concede that capabilities haven’t been substantially improving since their early days, and I don’t care for restating the whole argument here. My objective instead is to examine the nature of this frontier, and how it is likely to evolve over time to affect the way humans live and work in the economy of today and tomorrow.

As an intuition pump, think of compute as what expands the border, giving you more area to play with. Compute has been the single most reliable driver of progress since the dawn of the deep learning era; throwing more compute at a model, whether during training or at inference time, generally makes it better without regressing on things it could already do. Data determines the direction of that expansion, where “direction” encodes, in some loose sense, a particular domain, task, or capability. And algorithms are what enable the expansion to happen at all, in different ways depending on what they’re doing under the hood. Scaling all three has pushed the frontier outward dramatically, but it has not smoothed out the jaggedness. In fact, some recent developments have likely made it worse: reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, for example, makes the models much better at tasks that have, well, (automatically) verifiable rewards, but doesn’t seem to generalize very well, making the frontier even more spiky and hard to judge.

If you believe none of this matters—that superintelligence will arrive soon enough, surpassing humans at every cognitive task—then mapping the frontier matters less than working on superalignment or post-AGI policy. But if you think we have a good few years before then (or that true AGI isn’t coming in the current paradigm), it’s worth understanding the frontier’s shape as precisely as possible: why any given task is more or less automatable now and in the near future. The economic and political landscape at the “AGI moment” will, after all, be vastly shaped by who has already lost their job (or seen it dramatically overhauled) and how effective policy responses and forecasts will have been.

Sitting on the Bench

So how can we know where the frontier lies? A natural instinct is to look at benchmarks. But there’s a few, by-now-well-known problems with that. First off, benchmarks are at best a shadow of the real economy, and a partial one at that. Almost all benchmarks out there mostly test coding, math, or general knowledge. The chart below, borrowed from a fresh paper from CMU and Stanford, shows the stark mismatch: thousands of benchmark tasks target computer and mathematical occupations, while occupation groups that collectively employ tens of millions of workers and represent vast shares of GDP have essentially zero dedicated benchmark coverage.

Even setting this coverage gap aside, benchmarks are only imperfect windows into real-world job performance. Among several issues, they only test isolated skills under controlled conditions, which is not how work actually happens; their scores are vulnerable to data contamination and Goodharting, where models (or their developers) optimise for the metric itself rather than the underlying capability it was meant to proxy; they typically evaluate single-step, single-turn performance rather than the realistic sequential tasks that define most occupations; they often only measure whether a model can produce a correct output but rarely whether it does so reliably across repeated attempts, or what happens when it doesn’t; and they operate in a vacuum, where a task like “scheduling a meeting” might look trivial but would in practice require detailed knowledge of participants’ preferences and habits, organisational norms, and context-sensitive judgment calls. In short, benchmarks provide a signal, but there is too much noise for them to stand on their own as a reliable measure of AI’s actual progress in the workplace.

Exposing Exposure

If benchmarks can’t reliably map the frontier, what can? There are broadly two approaches that complement each other. The first is simply to look at data coming in from the workplace. Rather than trying to predict what AI could automate, you observe what it is automating, or at least where its effects are starting to show up in economic data. There are some early attempts at this kind of measurement, though most focus on aggregate indicators (think GDP growth, total factor productivity, broad employment trends) which are too coarse to tell us much about which specific tasks or roles are being affected. Ex-post analysis has a clear virtue, in that when it is done properly with fancy statistics and all, it’s pretty hard to dispute. But it also has an obvious limitation which is right there in the name: by the time the data confirms a trend, the trend has already occurred. This is useful for understanding the course we’re on, but it doesn’t quite help with forecasting, and forecasting is what policymakers, educators, and firms actually need if they want to anticipate the disruption rather than react to it.

The alternative is to try and get ahead of the curve. Instead of waiting for the data to arrive, you build a framework that tries to assess in advance how susceptible different tasks and occupations are to AI automation. Economists have come up with many such frameworks (in fact, quite possibly too many, and sometimes in disagreement with one another). There now exists a genuine plethora of exposure rubrics, each attempting to score how much each occupation is “exposed” to AI. This terminology is worth pausing on. Researchers use the word exposure rather than automation or displacement deliberately, because nobody wants to claim they’re making hard predictions about what will actually happen. Establishing that a task can be performed by an AI system, or will be performable soon, is very different from saying the task will be automated in practice, let alone that it will be automated consistently, at scale, across the whole economy. Exposure scores, then, are not forecasts, but a starting point: a structured assessment of technical susceptibility, and what happens downstream depends on economics, culture, and institutions. But you can’t model the downstream dynamics without first mapping the frontier, and that requires two things: a way of decomposing jobs into their constituent parts, and a way of scoring each part.

The most useful decomposition comes from task-based models, which have been the workhorse of labour economics since Autor, Levy, and Murnane, with important extensions by Acemoglu and Restrepo. The core idea is that a job is not a single entity that either gets automated or doesn’t, but is in fact a bundle of tasks, each playing a distinct role and therefore largely complementary to each other in the production process. A radiologist reads scans, consults with patients, writes reports, supervises technicians, and stays current with the literature; automation might handle some of these tasks well, others partially, and others not at all. There's a good argument that a task-level analysis is more appropriate to describe AI exposure than an occupation-level one, as several confounders emerge when determining overall impact on a profession. In fact, when one task is automated, the marginal value of adjacent human tasks can rise or fall in hard-to-predict ways: substitution reshuffles responsibilities, sometimes concentrating human effort on higher-judgment work, sometimes fragmenting it into supervisory busywork. O-ring dynamics can dominate, meaning that overall output quality is governed by the weakest link in the task chain regardless of how well the automated components perform. Handoff frictions between human and machine steps introduce coordination costs that didn't previously exist. And finally, learning spillovers across tasks mean that automating one can quietly erode competence on another—a radiologist who stops reading routine scans may lose exactly the pattern recognition that informs her clinical judgment on the difficult cases, making full automation of the "stepping stone" task unattractive even when technically possible.

Rubrics score exposure for each task (typically using the O*NET dataset). Here, the most influential recent contribution is by Eloundou et al., which built on earlier work by Brynjolfsson et al. on classifying tasks by their suitability for machine learning. The methodology, at a high level, works like this: take the O*NET database of occupational tasks, and for each task, ask whether it could be substantially accelerated by a GPT-4-class language model (>50% productivity boost), either directly or with the aid of near-future software tools built on top of one. Aggregate the task-level scores up to occupations, and voilà, you get an exposure profile for the entire economy. Their study was an important contribution, and it correctly anticipated several patterns that have since begun to show up in practice, but it also has limitations. In AI terms, it is quite ancient, as it was published in 2023, before the current wave of agentic systems, before tool use and multi-step planning became standard capabilities, and before code execution and computer use moved from prototypes to products. Its definition of exposure is in terms of uplift is a reasonable choice, as uplift is relatively experimentally accessible (though expensive to measure at scale), but it is also a static snapshot of what capabilities looked like at that specific time, rather than a time-varying forecast, as well as being intrinsically about augmentation rather than pure automatability.

More fundamentally, the rubric asks a binary question—is this task exposed or not?—without pausing to ask why. Two tasks can both be classified as “exposed” for different reasons, and those reasons have different implications for how quickly and completely automation will actually proceed. This is the gap that our research tries to fill. Rather than asking “is this task exposed?”, we ask along which dimensions, and we decompose exposure into a set of empirically motivated axes that capture the distinct reasons a task might resist automation.

A bottleneck-based approach

We begin by filtering O*NET tasks for digitability: whether a modern AI agent equipped with appropriate tools and permissions could in principle perform the task, allowing for minimal adaptation of platforms and workflows (e.g. favouring online over in-person meetings). Following Barnett, we use an LLM-as-judge to sort tasks into four buckets (digital, predominantly digital, predominantly non-digital, non-digital) and validate the classification against existing teleworkability scores. From this point on, "task" refers to anything in the first two categories.

Next, we run our judge models (GPT-5.3, Claude Sonnet 4.6, and Gemini 3.1 Flash) on every digital task, scoring each along twelve bottleneck dimensions:

  • Distributional Coverage: whether the task involves rare, private, or weakly represented instances that fall outside the distribution LLM pipelines were trained on.
  • Tacit Knowledge: the situational judgement and experiential calibration competent humans rely on but that can't be cleanly captured in rules or examples.
  • Specification Ambiguity: how much of the task's success criteria is implicit, context-dependent, or only revealed in retrospect, rather than something that can be written down up front.
  • Sequential Coherence: the planning horizon and multi-step reliability the task demands, where compounding errors and goal drift become limiting factors.
  • Working Memory: the volume and heterogeneity of information that must be held in mind and integrated within a single task episode.
  • Long-Term Memory: dependence on accumulated context across episodes (client history, project state, prior decisions) that current agent memory systems struggle to retain.
  • Multimodal Demand: reliance on perception, generation, or reasoning over non-text modalities such as images, audio, video, or spatial layouts.
  • Inference Speed: latency or throughput requirements that current LLM inference can't meet without unacceptable quality degradation.
  • Output Verifiability: the joint problem of how hard outputs are to check for errors and how often agents actually produce them.
  • Reliability Threshold: the accuracy and consistency the task's consequence profile demands, particularly where errors are severe or irreversible.
  • Counterpart Acceptance: resistance from the people on the other side of the task (clients, patients, colleagues) who would change their behaviour upon learning the performer is an AI.
  • Regulatory & Legal Constraints: the licensing, liability, and institutional permissions an AI agent would need in order to legally perform the task.

Each dimension is scored on two axes: relevance ($R_{td}$, how important the dimension $d$ is as a bottleneck for the task $t$) and severity ($B_{td}$, how large the gap is between current AI capability and what the task requires). Both use a discrete 0–2 scale:

Relevance $R_{td}$ Severity $B_{td}$
0Low0Absent
1Medium1Moderate
2High2Severe

We aggregate the per-dimension scores into a single task-level number using an $\varepsilon$-safe weighted geometric mean, where relevance acts as the weight on each severity term:

$$ s_t^{\,\mathrm{geom},\varepsilon} \;=\; \prod_{d=1}^{D}\!\left(\varepsilon + 1 - \frac{B_{td}}{2}\right)^{\!\tilde{w}_{td}}, \qquad \tilde{w}_{td} \;=\; \frac{\lambda_d\, R_{td}}{\sum_{j=1}^{D}\, \lambda_j\, R_{tj}}, \qquad \varepsilon = 0.01. $$

The $\lambda_d$ are task-independent dimension weights—global multipliers that let us emphasise or downweight particular bottlenecks across the whole task universe (for example, treating regulatory constraints as less binding than capability gaps). By default we set $\lambda_d = 1$ for all $d$, so that aggregation is driven entirely by per-task relevance $R_{td}$; the interactive dashboard exposes these weights as sliders so any reader can re-run the aggregation under their own assumptions.

This form respects the multiplicative logic of bottlenecks: a single severe gap on a highly relevant dimension can pull the aggregate score down sharply, even when every other dimension looks unconstrained. The $\varepsilon$ term simply prevents the product from collapsing to exactly zero when $B_{td}=2$, which would otherwise dominate the score regardless of any adequacy elsewhere.

Distributional Coverage

Machine learning, and therefore our rubric, begins with data. The first and most consequential stage is pretraining, the phase where the model ingests an enormous text corpus and learns, through next-token prediction, to compress the statistical structure of language into its weights. Pretraining is where the model acquires the vast majority of its knowledge: the model learns to predict what comes next, and in doing so, it learns syntax, facts, reasoning patterns, style, domain knowledge, and a great deal else, all entangled together in billions of parameters. A task that is well-represented in these online corpora is therefore more likely to fall inside the jagged frontier, but sheer volume isn’t all that matters: in order for the model to learn effectively, the data needs to be as close as possible to actual demonstrations, with step-by-step guides, worked examples, expert reasoning traces, instructional walkthroughs, and so on.

Take, for instance, mathematics. The ArXiv contains hundreds of thousands of papers, at first glance a veritable treasure trove. But maths papers are to actual mathematical research what a showroom car is to the garage where it was built: real research is a mess of blackboard scribbles, calculations in margins, half-baked conjectures tested and discarded, lengthy arguments with colleagues (and with yourself in the shower), dead ends pursued for weeks before being abandoned, almost none of which finds its way into the final paper, which is a polished artefact optimised for verification and citation rather than for teaching a naive learner how to do the thing (as many new grad students regret to find out). A model trained on the arXiv will know an extraordinary amount of mathematics, but generalising from polished proofs to the messy, creative process of producing new ones is a different challenge entirely, and one where the training data provides much weaker signal.

This is a thorny problem, because like most of science maths research frequently happens (almost by definition) out of distribution, with each new theorem potentially requiring novel insights beyond routine calculations. More broadly, we can think of an O*NET task as a superset of concrete instances. A task statement such as “coding” isn’t terribly informative of what your day at work might actually entail, since it could refer to anything from implementing a simple sorting function to refactoring a gigantic repository. The distribution of concrete instances for a given task may be fat-tailed, with long tails in every direction representing the chance that something comes out of left field to challenge you, or relatively peaked around a few typical, routine cases. The amount of quality of data relating to a task matters insofar as it covers a good chunk of this distribution. While there is still some debate surrounding the generalization abilities of LLMs, it is quite undeniable at this point that they are quite capable of solving unseen challenges (consider as evidence GPT’s and Gemini’s gold medals at the most recent IMO, or the fact that such models are being more and more often used to derive novel results in physics and mathematics); however, generalisation cannot happen when the distance between training and test distributions is insurmountably large.

If ample, high-quality demonstrations of a task are not already available online, not all hope is lost. Through supervised fine-tuning, patches can be applied to uncovered regions, provided it is possible to construct (label, example) pairs either synthetically or by hiring human experts. In some domains reinforcement learning also helps, wherever human feedback is effective or rewards can be assigned automatically and cheaply on scalably generated tasks. Reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards is, in fact, exactly what unlocked the recent large improvements in mathematics and coding for reasoning models, leading to a proliferation of startups that supply labs with RL environments and reward signals for specific, economically valuable tasks. Therefore, the more relevant question for our rubric is: given the demonstrations present in the pretraining corpora, together with targeted post-training, is the distribution of task instances sufficiently well-represented to allow the model to handle the bulk of what a human worker would encounter in practice?

Tacit Knowledge

While some tasks are challenging because of insufficient data, others may not admit usable data even in principle, at least not in a format digestible by language models, however heavily augmented. The obstacle is tacit knowledge. Tacit knowledge lives in the crooks and nannies between words, that resists extraction and articulation in verbal or written form, and that can often be gained only through direct experience. Intuition, insight, and seasoned judgment all fall under this umbrella. Codified knowledge (procedures, rules, factual relationships, and so on) is precisely what language models can acquire at scale through pretraining and fine-tuning; tacit knowledge is, almost by definition, what they do not.

The idea was first formalised in a seminal philosophical treatise by Polanyi, who critiqued what he called the “ideal of impersonal, detached objectivity” in science: even scientific discovery, he argued, depends on hunches, aesthetic judgments, and skilled perception that cannot be reduced to explicit method. The point generalises beyond science. For many economically valuable tasks, competent performance depends on pattern recognition, situational calibration, and context-sensitive judgment that language captures only partially, if at all. Repeatedly performing a task makes you much better at it, even if you have carefully read and memorised a tutorial beforehand, in ways that would often be hard to retell someone else. Multimodal data might help narrow the gap, but only to an extent—after all a picture may be worth a thousand words, but experience is the teacher whose lessons actually stick.

Tacit knowledge barriers have been surmounted before, typically through self-play and autonomous exploration rather than pure imitation learning. Early approaches to teaching games such as chess or go to machines relied heavily on hand-crafted examples and expert demonstrations, much as early vision models relied on manual feature engineering. It was only when systems were allowed to learn from experience by playing millions of games against themselves that they broke through, with the added benefit of not being bounded by the ceiling of human performance, as AlphaGo’s move 37 memorably demonstrated to the world.

Can reinforcement learning always compensate? Not necessarily, due to the problem of reward specification. RL can help a model internalise judgment, but only when the relevant discrimination can be exposed through a sufficiently faithful reward signal or evaluator. Where the tacit component is sparse, delayed, normative, or only partially observable, RL does not so much remove the bottleneck as relocate it into the equally difficult problem of designing the reward function itself. Tacit knowledge thus functions both as a ceiling on what text-centric pretraining and instruction-based supervision can achieve alone, and as a filter on where experiential training methods can make further inroads.

In the absence of full-blown experiential learning, a number of proposals are emerging to capture as much tacit knowledge as possible at the source. One approach involves equipping willing laboratories (who are not traditionally difficult to convince, provided a sufficiently generous grant lands in their coffers) with microphones, cameras, and sensors on every instrument, recording the messy, unscripted reality of research as it happens. The corporate analogue is, if anything, easier to imagine and more Orwellian in flavour: every meeting, call, click, and draft monitored and recorded, compressed into training data for the automated workers and scientists of the future. How much tacit knowledge such surveillance-by-design can actually recover remains speculative—it is one thing to record a master surgeon’s hand movements, quite another to distill from the footage whatever it is that makes them a master… but it likely won’t stop the labs (or someone else for them) from trying.

Long-Term Memory

Whether knowledge is acquired from experience or from text-based training, it counts for precious little if it is promptly forgotten. Under the current transformer-based paradigm, memory does not persist beyond the context window a language model enjoys in a single session; once that window closes, the state is wiped clean. Any task that requires keeping track of information across sessions quickly runs into this problem.

Examples of this cut across nearly every occupation. A financial adviser who cannot recall a client’s risk tolerance from a previous meeting is not much of an adviser; an employee who receives explicit feedback from a manager is expected to retain and act on it; a recurring customer’s preferences, either explicit or inferred, should inform decisions in all subsequent iterations. Much of what makes someone good at their job is not only raw skill but accumulated context: preferences revealed over time, lessons extracted from past mistakes, and relationships built through repeated interaction.

Fine-tuning can help to some extent: a model can be periodically retrained on new data to absorb updated knowledge. But even when possible, fine-tuning is expensive and haunted by the problem of catastrophic forgetting, the well-documented tendency for neural networks to overwrite what they previously knew when trained on new information. This fragility has led some in the field—Dwarkesh Patel most famously—to flag memory as one of the principal reasons for near-term scepticism about AI’s economic impact. The concern has since helped elevate continual learning (or online or lifelong learning) into one of the field’s most active research frontiers.

It is worth remembering, however, that human memory is also deeply imperfect, perhaps instructively so. We do not record experience verbatim. The same reward signals that drive learning also shape which memories are consolidated and which are discarded: a piece of feedback that carries real consequences is far more likely to stick than one delivered in passing. Human memory is much closer to lossy compression, shaped by relevance and reinforcement, than a literal transcript.

Where biological memory falls short, people compensate with external tools—from notebooks and calendars to CRMs and institutional documentation. Language model agents can do the same, and many already do: Claude Code, for instance, maintains a CLAUDE.md file that functions as a persistent scratchpad, allowing the model to read and update its own notes across sessions. But the approach has limits: deciding what to write down, how to organise it, and when to retrieve it are judgment calls that often circle back to the problem of tacit knowledge. A seasoned employee knows instinctively which details from a meeting are worth remembering and which can be safely forgotten; an agent writing to a memory file must make that same discrimination explicitly, without the benefit of the intuition that makes it easy. External memory, in other words, does not eliminate the problem so much as reframe it: the bottleneck shifts from storage to curation, and curation is hard to do well without the very contextual judgment that memory was supposed to provide.

Sequential Coherence

In March 2025, the research nonprofit METR asked the question: how long a task can an AI model actually do? To answer that, they built a suite of software engineering challenges spanning everything from seconds-long fixes to multi-hour projects, timed skilled developers on each one, ran frontier models through the same tasks, and fitted a curve. The point on the time axis where a model’s success rate drops to 50% is what they call its time horizon. The headline result wasn’t the number itself (which comes with error bars wide enough to drive a truck through) but the trend line: frontier time horizons have been doubling roughly every seven months since 2019, and recent models suggest the pace may be picking up.

The METR framework has since become something of a lodestar for AI forecasting, playing a central role in modelling efforts by groups like the AI Futures Project and the OECD foresight team. The logic is straightforward: if you can assign a time horizon to every task in a task-based economic model, you can extrapolate from the METR trend to estimate when each task will fall within reach of automated systems; as the trend line marches upward, the pool of vulnerable tasks expands on a predictable schedule.

What the time horizon proxies is two distinct sources of difficulty, entangled in a single number. The first is something we might call sequential coherence, a concept explored in this LessWrong post which defined it as “an AI system’s ability to maintain consistent performance quality across extended sequences of steps or interactions.” Current AI systems can often solve individual problems impressively, but performance tends to degrade as you chain more steps together, not because each step gets harder, but because the probability of failing at some step compounds; a model might be perfectly capable of performing each step of a complex workflow but fail catastrophically when those steps need to be chained together over an extended sequence with no human checkpoint in between. The second source of difficulty is per-step depth: how hard any individual step is in isolation, independent of how many steps surround it. A task can take hours not because it involves a long chain of operations, but because one step requires searching a vast space of approaches with little intermediate feedback before finding the right insight or creative leap. These two dimensions are largely independent and imply different automation trajectories. Sequential coherence problems yield to better scaffolding and error recovery; per-step depth problems require raw improvements in reasoning capability, and no amount of decomposition helps, because it is difficult to decompose an insight into sub-insights.

In the rubric, we only consider sequential coherence and use time horizons as an acceptable proxy of it for most jobs, due to the fact that it is more predictable, easier to measure, and dominant in most work.

Working Memory

If long-term memory is about retaining lessons across sessions, working memory is about holding enough information within a single session to reason effectively over it. For transformer-based language models, this capacity is defined by the context window, the maximum number of tokens the model can attend to at once. Everything the model needs to know in order to respond must fit inside this window; anything that falls outside it simply does not exist for the model.

Context windows have expanded dramatically over the years. In 2018, GPT-1 operated with 512 tokens, roughly a page of text. By early 2023, most models worked with somewhere between 4,000 and 8,000 tokens. Then the pace accelerated: 128,000 tokens with GPT-4 Turbo, 200,000 with Claude 3, a million with Gemini 1.5 Pro, and by 2025, claims of up to ten million tokens from Meta’s Llama 4 Scout. Epoch AI estimates that since mid-2023, the longest context windows have been growing at roughly 30x per year. A million tokens is approximately 750,000 words, enough to ingest an entire codebase, a shelf of legal contracts, or several novels in a single pass.

But context windows are not free, and scaling them is harder than the headline numbers suggest. In a standard transformer, every token must attend to every other token, producing a computational cost that scales quadratically with sequence length: doubling the context window quadruples the compute required. What’s more is that tokens do not straightforwardly mean better performance, as the well-known “lost in the middle” phenomenon shows: models attend strongly to information placed at the beginning and end of the context but perform markedly worse on information placed in the middle. And beyond positional effects, there is the simpler problem of attention dilution: as the context fills with more tokens, the model’s finite attention budget is spread thinner, and information that would have been highly salient in a short context can become functionally invisible in a long one.

Modern agentic systems deal with these limitations through compression, the digital analogue of cognitive load management. Claude Code, for instance, employs a multi-layered compaction strategy: as the context fills during a long session, the system first trims verbose tool outputs, then summarises older conversation turns, and finally, if needed, compresses the entire history into a condensed summary that strives to preserve key decisions and the current state of work.

The analogy to human cognition is imperfect but useful. A person working on a complex project does not hold every detail in active attention simultaneously; instead, they maintain a compressed, prioritised representation of the overall state with the gist, the open questions, the immediate next steps, and reach for specific details only when needed, by re-reading a document, checking a notebook, or asking a colleague. Compaction in AI agents is an engineered approximation of this process: the system decides what is worth retaining in the active summary and what can be offloaded or discarded, gambling that the summary preserves enough signal for continued effective work. The gamble does not always pay off. Users of long-running Claude Code sessions report a characteristic failure mode: after compaction, the agent “forgets” critical constraints established early in the conversation (architectural decisions, access control rules, stylistic preferences) because the summarisation process judged them less salient than more recent activity.

The upshot is that working memory remains a genuine bottleneck, even as context windows expand. Tasks that require integrating evidence scattered across a long context, holding multiple constraints in tension, or maintaining coherence across a complex multi-step plan all push against these limits in ways that raw token counts do not capture. For any task where the relevant working state exceeds what can be reliably held and manipulated within a single context window, performance will degrade; the longer and more complex the task, the more severe the degradation is likely to be.

Multimodal Requirements

Although new paradigms for natively multimodal pretraining are emerging—training on interleaved text, image, video, and action-conditioned data from the ground up rather than bolting perceptual modules onto a text-pretrained backbone—the vast majority of frontier model capability still derives from text-only pretraining. This wouldn’t matter much if every job were a text-in-text-out affair, but a great number of occupations, digital or not, require engaging with the visual or auditory world—from an orthopedic surgeon examining an x-ray to an audio engineer mixing a track or a film editor cutting a scene. These tasks demand perceptual and generation abilities that current models may not always match.

Not all modalities are equal. In terms of perception, vision-language is pretty mature: models can describe images and answer questions about them, often matching human accuracy; audio is moving fast too, with transcription and voice synthesis already good enough for production in many cases. Video is the laggard: tracking objects across frames, understanding temporal structure, generating coherent clips are all improving quickly, but not where they need to be for a lot of real-world tasks.

When it comes to generation, models have come a long way since the days of stable diffusion, but the outputs often betray small imperfections that a skilled human would not ship (the odd fifth finger here or there, contextual inaccuracies). Cross-modality reasoning is another weak spot, especially anything involving spatial understanding or 3D structure: models that can talk fluently about an image still struggle to reason about how objects relate in three-dimensional space, which makes tasks like 3D modelling, architectural layout, or arranging elements in a physical scene harder than might be expected based on a model’s general reasoning capabilities.

The arc from early vision-language adapters to natively multimodal systems like Gemini’s integrated image generation shows both how fast things are moving and how far there is to go. We’re shifting from systems that bolt on non-text modalities as an afterthought to ones designed to think in images, sounds, and text all at once—but we’re not there yet, and for any task where the core difficulty is in a non-text modality that gap might be the thing that holds everything up.

Inference Speed

Today’s frontier systems operate along an explicit latency–quality tradeoff. Lightweight models provide fast responses at lower cost, while reasoning-oriented systems improve quality by spending more inference-time computation, generating internal chains of thought, calling tools, and deliberating across multiple steps before producing a final answer. Vendors increasingly expose this tradeoff directly: model routing architectures allocate incoming queries between fast-response and deeper-reasoning systems based on estimated difficulty, and many providers offer a reasoning effort parameter that lets users dial the tradeoff for a single model: set it to “low” and the model spends fewer tokens on internal deliberation, responding quickly but with less thorough reasoning; set it to “high” and it allocates substantially more computation to its chain of thought before answering.

In general, it is possible to obtain equivalent model performance either by spending more compute at training time (producing a more capable model that can answer cheaply) or by spending more at inference time (using a cheaper model that thinks harder per query). The two are, within limits, substitutable: roughly one order of magnitude of compute can be shifted in either direction across several techniques—chain of thought, repeated sampling, model scaling, pruning—all of which can often be stacked. The relationship between capability and cost is hence not fixed at the moment of deployment. A task that is prohibitively expensive to automate today, because it requires an expensive frontier model running at high reasoning effort, may become cheap tomorrow, not because the task got easier, but because improvements in inference efficiency, model distillation, or architecture shifts reduced the compute required to reach the same quality threshold (in fact, Epoch estimates that the cost to reach a fixed level of LLM performance has been falling by roughly two orders of magnitude per year).

This matters for our framework because a task’s automation exposure depends not only on whether a model can perform it reliably, but also on whether successful performance can be achieved within the response-time window the task allows. At one end are fully asynchronous tasks where minutes or hours of deliberation per step are acceptable or even expected, such as report writing, research analysis, document review, due diligence; at the other end are tasks that require rapid turn-taking or immediate reaction, such as simultaneous interpretation, live customer interactions, or real-time operational control. Tasks with loose latency budgets are more exposed to progress in inference-time scaling, because models can spend substantial computation per step without violating user expectations. Tasks with tight latency budgets are more resistant, not necessarily because they require less intelligence, but because they leave little room to buy quality through slower reasoning.

This is especially binding for tasks that require both high quality and low latency simultaneously. Progress on these tasks depends more heavily on improvements in inference efficiency, hardware acceleration, and architectural innovation than on test-time scaling alone. Diffusion language models, which generate multiple tokens in parallel via iterative denoising rather than one at a time, represent one such architectural bet: recent work has demonstrated inference speeds exceeding those of comparably sized autoregressive models, though the quality–speed tradeoff remains an active area of research and the ecosystem is far less mature. Speculative decoding, multi-token prediction, and quality-preserving distillation of reasoning into smaller, faster models all push in the same direction, collapsing the latency cost of intelligence and thereby expanding the set of tasks for which automation becomes practical within real-world time constraints.

Assurance Threshold

AI models fail often, but unfortunately for us humans do as well all too often. In many low-stakes situations it is possible, within reasonable limits, to simply try again; in others, less so. METR reports their time horizons at a given reliability level, with the absolute value of those horizons dropping considerably for every boost in reliability. The headline horizon is at 50% reliability, meaning that the models will succeed at tasks that are at the frontier of their capabilities roughly half of the time. But is 50% reliability on a given task enough? That depends largely on how consequential a potential error is. The key questions are: Can you retry, or do errors propagate instantly? If retry is possible, how easily can the error actually be caught in time? And if it isn’t caught in time, what happens?

For an AI that drafts internal meeting summaries, a few mistakes are a minor annoyance. But for an AI making triage decisions in an emergency department, or flagging transactions in a systemic-risk context, the tolerance for failure drops to near zero. We therefore define an assurance threshold linked to how impactful errors tend to be. This is closely related to the O*NET “consequence of error” variable, although it is scored at the level of individual tasks rather than for the full occupation. This reflects the fact that even within a stereotypically highly consequential occupation, different tasks have different risk profiles: in the case of a medical doctor, prescribing medication has high modal risk (a wrong dosage or drug interaction can cause direct harm), while logging patient data mostly leads to minor clerical errors, but some could be catastrophic, like recording the wrong disease, leading to a misguided treatment plan downstream.

This example also points to the need of further discriminating between modal and tail risk, the former capturing the typical severity when something goes wrong and the latter the rare but extreme failures. A task can have low modal risk yet serious tail risk, and systems need to be evaluated against both.

Specification ambiguity

No matter how intelligent an agent, it won’t start taking actions until prompted—at least for now. This means that a human overseer’s ability to specify what they want the agent to do remains a binding constraint on automation. Some tasks are easy to specify (“translate this paragraph into French”), others are nebulous, carrying most of their meaning beneath the surface. They often have implicit objectives, contextual success criteria, and constraints the requester wouldn’t think to articulate (or even be capable of expressing) until they’ve been violated. Besides limiting performance, high specification ambiguity opens the door to specification gaming, where an agent optimises for the letter of the prompt while violating its spirit. This is a well-documented failure mode, and one that may grow worse as agents are increasingly trained through reinforcement learning.

In the near future, agents may run in continuous loops, absorbing context, making judgment calls, and initiating actions without waiting for a fresh human prompt. Orchestrators of agents might themselves be AIs, much as human managers oversee other humans. Even then, specification ambiguity doesn’t necessarily disappear: the ambiguity that once sat between a user and a tool now sits between an AI principal and an AI subordinate, raising the same questions at a different level of abstraction: can the delegating agent specify what it wants clearly enough for the executing agent to do the right thing? Or is the subordinate agent capable enough to infer the latent goal and pursue it autonomously?

The problem is especially important for agentic systems because autonomy increases the cost of misunderstood intent. A chatbot that misinterprets a prompt produces a bad answer; an agent that misinterprets a prompt may execute a bad plan, spend money, contact other people, modify systems, or lock in errors across multiple steps before a human notices. In that sense, greater autonomy does not relax the specification bottleneck and the more discretion an agent has, the more important it becomes that the agent can infer the latent objective rather than merely parse the literal instruction.

Stakeholder Acceptance

I don’t think an AI would be able to sell my grandpa a vacuum cleaner. Not necessarily because the AI couldn’t deliver a good pitch (after all, research has shown that language models are more persuasive than people), but because my grandpa would hang up the moment he realised he wasn’t talking to a person. A sale that the counterparty refuses to engage with has failed from the start, regardless of how skilled the salesman might be. This is a problem that cuts across a surprising number of occupations: even when an LLM-based agent can perform a task competently, deployment may flounder if the humans on the other side of the interaction won’t play along.

The degree to which human resistance to interacting with an AI agent degrades a task’s outcomes net of whatever the agent’s demonstrated quality can do to overcome that resistance is what we call the sociotechnical bottleneck of counterpart acceptance. We’re not trying to measure public opinion about AI, or aggregate trust surveys, or whether people tell pollsters they’d prefer a human. The question is whether, for a specific task in a specific occupational context, counterpart behaviour would materially change in ways that undermine the task’s objectives if the counterpart knew the performer was an AI.

In fact, what people say and what people do are often wildly different things. Patients express discomfort with AI-generated medical advice while relying on symptom-checkers every time they feel a twinge. Consumers report distrust of algorithmic recommendations while exhibiting identical purchasing behaviour to those who were never told an algorithm was involved. The literature on algorithm aversion has documented in detail the tendency to abandon an algorithmic forecaster after seeing it err, even when it outperforms human forecasters, but so has the opposite phenomenon, algorithm appreciation, where people in certain contexts actually defer more to algorithmic than to human advice. The only way to cut through it is to focus on what people actually do when they encounter an AI in a specific task, not what they say they’d do in the abstract.

Several things are in play. The most direct mechanism is legitimacy withdrawal: counterparts refuse to accept decisions or advice from a non-human because the social role demands a personally accountable human. A patient might reject a therapeutic recommendation not because it is wrong but because it comes from a machine; a subordinate might disregard automated performance feedback not because it is inaccurate but because being evaluated by a machine feels demeaning in a way that being evaluated by a mediocre human boss somehow does not. The authority to judge, advise, or decide is, in many contexts, inseparable from the perception that the entity doing it has something at stake. A subtler problem is strategic adaptation: when people know they are dealing with an AI, the cooperative fiction that underlies many professional interactions (the assumption that both parties are playing by the same social rules) can quietly collapse. Negotiators adopt extreme anchoring tactics they would never try with a human, students game an AI tutor’s feedback loops rather than actually learning. Then there is perhaps the hardest problem: some tasks require not just competent performance but the perception of being heard or cared about, and they lose their effectiveness when performed by an agent that counterparts believe feels nothing.

The trajectory of this bottleneck is among the most uncertain in the rubric. Acceptance is normalising fast in some domains while remaining deeply entrenched in others, and the variation across jurisdictions, demographics, and generations is enormous. A grandpa is not an early adopter, but today’s youngest generations might not share his reservations, and the line between “I’d never trust a machine with that” and “wait, that was a machine?” has a way of moving faster than anyone expects.

Regulatory & Legal

While the preceding dimensions ask whether an LLM-based agent can perform a task competently, this one asks a prior, upstream question: whether the agent is permitted to perform it at all. It doesn’t matter if a system can flawlessly draft a will, read a radiograph, or approve a mortgage application if the law requires a licensed attorney, a board-certified radiologist, or a credentialed loan officer to sign off.

The most straightforward constraint is professional licensure. A number of occupational tasks are legally reserved for holders of specific credentials, and in virtually every jurisdiction these credentials are defined as attributes of natural persons. An LLM cannot hold a medical license, cannot be admitted to the bar, and cannot stamp engineering drawings. The constraint is binary in principle but admits workarounds in practice: a radiologist who reviews and countersigns an AI-generated read is operating within the law; the same AI generating the same read without the radiologist is not. Even under strong regulation, augmentation would likely still be possible for many such tasks, but not full automation.

A broader constraint is mandatory human decision-making. The EU AI Act requires meaningful human oversight for consequential decisions in employment, credit, law enforcement, and education. US financial regulation requires that adverse credit decisions be explainable by an accountable decision-maker. Administrative law in multiple jurisdictions prohibits delegating discretionary governmental authority to automated systems without human review. In all such cases, AI can contribute to the decision (and it increasingly does) but cannot be the final decision-maker.

Even where no explicit prohibition exists, the absence of a viable liability framework can be just as effective at blocking deployment. The age-old question, being asked since the early days of autonomous vehicles, is: If an AI agent causes harm, who bears responsibility—the developer, the deployer, the supervising professional, or the end user? In most jurisdictions, for most task types, the answer is unclear, and uncertainty about liability functions may sometimes count as de facto prohibition, as risk-averse organisations might not deploy agents for consequential tasks if they cannot determine who will be holding the bag when something goes wrong. In the case of self-driving cars, a major deployment bottleneck is not the technical ability to drive but the legal system’s ability to decide who was at fault when things go wrong.

Finally, there is sector-specific regulation. Agencies like the FDA, FAA, and SEC impose domain-specific requirements on how tasks must be performed, documented, and audited, and such requirements are designed around human performers and institutional workflows. Adapting these frameworks to accommodate autonomous AI may require rulemaking processes that are slow, contested, and jurisdiction-specific: the FDA is not going to wake up one morning and decide that an AI can approve a drug application. That will unfold over years, possibly decades, at different speeds across sectors and countries.
